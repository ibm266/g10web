## **Book the Right Time of Day (This One Matters Most)**

The single biggest thing you can control is when we shoot. Aruba sun is gorgeous, but it's strong, and harsh midday light means squinting, sweating, and washed-out skies. None of that helps anyone relax.

I almost always book families for the hour after sunrise or the hour before sunset, what photographers call golden hour. The light is soft, warm, and flattering, and it's cooler out, which matters a lot when little ones are involved. As an [**_Aruba family photographer_**](https://www.g10.studio/photography/family-in-aruba), I'd rather have you fresh and comfortable than melting at noon. Early mornings are also quieter on the beach, so you're not dodging crowds in the background of every frame.

If you're choosing between sunrise and sunset, think about your kids. Are they happiest first thing in the morning, or after a slow day and a good nap? Pick the window that fits your family's rhythm, not the other way around.

## **Dress for Comfort and Cohesion, Not Matching Outfits**

Forget the matching white-shirts-and-khakis look. It can read a little stiff, and stiff is the opposite of what we want. Instead, pick a small palette of two or three colors that work together and let everyone wear something within it. Soft, earthy tones and gentle blues photograph beautifully against the Aruba water and sand.

**A few things that help:**

  * Choose outfits that everyone can actually move in, so kids aren't tugging at collars the whole time.

  * Avoid big logos or busy patterns that pull the eye away from your faces.

  * Bring a backup outfit for the youngest ones (sand and snacks happen).

  * Let kids have a small say in what they wear so they feel comfortable and confident.




When everyone feels good in what they're wearing, it shows. That ease is what separates a posed photo from a real one.

## **Let Go of "Perfect" and Plan for Real Moments**

Here's the secret most families don't expect: the photos you'll love most usually aren't the ones where everyone's looking at the camera. They're the in-between moments, the dad tickling his kid, the grandma laughing, the toddler running into the waves.

My style is a mix of documentary and editorial, which is a fancy way of saying I'll pose you for a few show-stoppers, but mostly I'll let your family just be your family while I capture it. So go in to have fun together, not nailing a perfect formation. The [**best family pictures photographer**](/blog/family-photography-services-in-aruba) for you is one who lets your real personalities come through, not one who marches you through a stiff shot list.

If your toddler has a meltdown halfway through, that's completely fine. We take a break, grab a snack, and pick back up. I've never had a session where a little chaos ruined the photos. Usually, it gives us the funniest, most genuine ones.

### **A Quick Pre-Shoot Checklist**

To keep things easy, here's what I'd run through the day before:

  * Confirm the meeting spot and time, and build in a few extra minutes for parking and walking.

  * Lay out everyone's outfits the night before so the morning isn't a scramble.

  * Pack water, snacks, and a small toy or two for younger kids.

  * Get a good night's sleep; relaxed parents make for relaxed kids.

  * Come ready to have fun. That's honestly the only rule that matters.

## **Pick a Location That Fits Your Family, Not Just the View**

Everyone pictures the classic Aruba beach shot, and yes, we'll get those. But the right spot depends on who's in the photo. A session with a newborn and grandparents needs a calm, easy-to-reach beach with somewhere to sit. A group of active kids does better somewhere they can run around without me chasing them into the ocean.

I shoot all over the island, from the soft sand on the calmer western beaches to the more dramatic, rugged spots if you want something with a bit of edge. When we chat before the session, tell me about your family: ages, energy levels, and anyone with mobility needs. From there, I'll match you to a location that looks incredible and actually works for your group, so nobody's struggling across rocks in flip-flops.

### **Keep the Little Ones Happy (the Whole Session Depends on It)**

If the kids are happy, the photos are happy. It's that simple. The fastest way to derail a shoot is a hungry, tired, or overwhelmed little one, so a bit of planning here goes a long way.

**A few things that work every time:**

  * Feed them before we start, a snack in the car beats a meltdown on the beach.

  * Don't over-hype the shoot for days beforehand; keep it low-key so it feels like play.

  * Let them warm up to me for a few minutes before any photos. I'll usually crack a joke or two first.

  * Bring a small bribe for the end
